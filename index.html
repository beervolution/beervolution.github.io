The Early Days - Ancient Iran

Between 3500 and 3100 BC

The earliest chemically confirmed barley beer dates from between 3500 and 3100 BC and was discovered at Godin Tepe in the Central Zagros Mountains of Iran.

The Early Days - Ancient Egypt

Between 3500 and 3100 BC

Beer had an important role in Ancient Egypt and its manufacturing process was strictly controlled. Beer was for the higher class and was used as an offering to the gods. It was also prescribed to treat various illnesses.

Beer in Greece

Between 3300 to 1200 BC

It was believed that during the time of Ancient Greece, wine was consumed instead of beer. Only recently did archaeologists discover the remains of two prehistoric buildings, providing materials to which can be used for the development and consumption of beer

Beer in Germany

800 BC

The Teutons, the ancient Germans, regarded beer as a sacrifice to the gods. They started producing the first proof beer in the early Hallstatt Period.

Middle Ages

Between 500 to 1500

In the Middle Ages, Christian monks began to take charge of beer brewing to serve it with their meals. Monasteries became the repositories of brewing knowledge and skill and maintained knowledge of brewing and guarded their secrets. Monks brewed virtually all beer of good quality until the twelfth century. The successful history of beer in early Christianity depended heavily on the Church and its institutions.

16th Century

The history of beer in the 16th century was a period of increased regulation to ensure purity of beer, income of workers, fire safety, and high tax revenues.

-Extra: In Coventry, the average amount of beer and ale consumed was about 17 pints per person per week. Compared to the current recommendation of 6 pints per week.

17th Century

The history of beer in the 17th century had improved transportation which meant that beer could be shipped to as far as the New World. There were also both legal changes and technical innovations.

-Extra: The crew of the Mayflower did not have enough beer to get to Virginia and back to England. So they dropped the Pilgrims at Plymouth Rock to preserve their beer stock.

18th Century

The history of beer in the 18th century is largely one of innovation. With the invention of the steam engine in 1765, industrialization of beer became a reality. Greater attention was also focused on improving brewing methods during the century.

19th Century

The history of beer in the 19th century introduced thermometers and hydrometers led to major improvements in the brewing process. This paved the way for the modern era of brewing - commercial refrigeration, automatic bottling, pasteurization, and railroad distribution.

-Extra: Creation of Oktoberfest in 1810 (world’s largest Volksfest)

20th Century and Beyond

The history of beer in the 20th century saw the laws, technology, scientific advancement, business organization, and many other changes occurred. They influenced brewing techniques, brewery size, drinking tastes, and drinking patterns. Alternate beer such as 0% beer was also introduced.

Social Media provide exposure to 2 key aspects of beer consumption such as:
- Peer Alcohol Behaviour
- Alcohol Advertising

Content posted by adolescents and young adults are likely to be seen by their peers as well as younger users on social media.

Although there are restrictions in place to protect young people from exposure to alcohol advertisements on traditional media channels (television programs), adolescents still have access to alcohol advertising in many other avenues.

In particular, social media platforms such as Instagram and Tiktok present avenues for alcohol advertisers as they can target messages and foster connections with consumers. This is particularly concerning because it can easily reach adolescents and young adults under the legal drinking age who widely uses these social media platforms.

Economic Impacts

The beer industry is a vital engine of the economy as it helps with job creation. It is estimated that for every job at a brewer, 29 additional jobs are supported down or upstream and another 10 more through induced consumer spending channel. In fact, 1 in every 110 jobs in the world is linked through direct, indirect, or induced impact channels to the beer sector from farming, delivery to retail and on-premise sales, supporting a total of 23 million jobs.

With over 50 billion gallons of beer consumed each year, the total economic impact from the beer sector alone amounts to $555 billion of gross value added to the global GDP.

In 2019, before Covid upended the industry, the beer sector alone supported 23 million jobs from farming to delivery to retail and on-premise sales. However, due to the pandemic, the beer industry was severely impacted with reports suggesting an estimated decline of 12% in large brewers’ revenues. However, improvements can be seen in 2021 as the numbers went down.

Technological Impacts

The craft beer industry is one of the most tech-fueled businesses in the world today, with several production-related pieces of tech that came to the forefront of the beer landscape.

Beer canning was introduced in 1935 which changed the industry and the way we consume beer today in contrast to the olden days of old oak barrels and bottled beer. The process of canning beer is a 7-step process that once required significant investment and manpower. However, technological advancements through the use of computing and AI has increased the efficiency of this process with greater focus on expelling the air from the can through the use of Nitrogen canning.

More technological advancements shaping the beer industry can also be seen from the project IntelligentX which uses AI and machine learning to understand the individual taste of its users, creating the world’s first beer brewed by AI. Based on the algorithm from its intelligence, it has initially started off with 4 flavours. However, it has since already been changed and enhanced 11 times based on consumers feedback.

Sustainability: Eco-friendly production
(1) Brewing Process
(2) Beer Packaging

1) Brewing Process: Sustainability concerns regarding the water use of more than 20:1 litre of brewed beer, energy use and waste generation of 85% of by products in the brewing process.

Technological solutions include:
- Water treatment and exploring ways to recycle waste water into beer.
- Repurposing it into food by using wastewater to create single-cell proteins that are suitable for feeding animals like fishes.

2) Beer Packaging: Research on life-cycle and greenhouse gas emissions of beer packaging materials have shown no definite conclusion on whether cans or bottles are environmentally superior. While bottles may be better in ameliorating the heavy impacts of mining bauxite for virgin aluminium to make cans, cans on the other hand, has a higher recycling rate containing approximately 68% recycled content and lower greenhouse gas emissions.

Conclusion

The individual consumption of alcohol is a personal choice

We examine the implications of beer, not just on the consumer, but also on the producer. We realise the health risks of beer consumption, but we also note the ease and affordability of procuring a beer due to the lowered costs of production from the effects of competition within the industry.

The application of beer in our society cannot be discounted as well. Our lives are, seemingly, intertwined with beer in so many different ways that we might not even have noticed.

Impacts of beer on all forms of life. Social, economic, and technological. In all fairness, beer brings about not only the negative effects that we tend to associate with, but definitely economical and technological effects too. It is unfair to generalise the negative effects of beer as consumption and tolerance of individuals varies, leading to different magnitudes of impact as well. We do not wish to fall into the idea of false dichotomy.

We bring back the point of the individual’s responsibility of the consumption of beer. As one would understand, from our page, the various negative, positive, or neutral impacts, implications and applications of beer, that beer has definitely seeped deep into our lives. It is up to the individual to see to it, the scale of consumption, to behave responsibly as proper human beings.
