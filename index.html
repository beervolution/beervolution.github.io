The Early Days - Ancient Iran

Between 3500 and 3100 BC

The earliest chemically confirmed barley beer dates from between 3500 and 3100 BC and was discovered at Godin Tepe in the Central Zagros Mountains of Iran.

The Early Days - Ancient Egypt

Between 3500 and 3100 BC

Beer had an important role in Ancient Egypt and its manufacturing process was strictly controlled. Beer was for the higher class and was used as an offering to the gods. It was also prescribed to treat various illnesses.

Beer in Greece

Between 3300 to 1200 BC

It was believed that during the time of Ancient Greece, wine was consumed instead of beer. Only recently did archaeologists discover the remains of two prehistoric buildings, providing materials to which can be used for the development and consumption of beer

Beer in Germany

800 BC

The Teutons, the ancient Germans, regarded beer as a sacrifice to the gods. They started producing the first proof beer in the early Hallstatt Period.

Middle Ages

Between 500 to 1500

In the Middle Ages, Christian monks began to take charge of beer brewing to serve it with their meals. Monasteries became the repositories of brewing knowledge and skill and maintained knowledge of brewing and guarded their secrets. Monks brewed virtually all beer of good quality until the twelfth century. The successful history of beer in early Christianity depended heavily on the Church and its institutions.

16th Century

The history of beer in the 16th century was a period of increased regulation to ensure purity of beer, income of workers, fire safety, and high tax revenues.

-Extra: In Coventry, the average amount of beer and ale consumed was about 17 pints per person per week. Compared to the current recommendation of 6 pints per week.

17th Century

The history of beer in the 17th century had improved transportation which meant that beer could be shipped to as far as the New World. There were also both legal changes and technical innovations.

-Extra: The crew of the Mayflower did not have enough beer to get to Virginia and back to England. So they dropped the Pilgrims at Plymouth Rock to preserve their beer stock.

18th Century

The history of beer in the 18th century is largely one of innovation. With the invention of the steam engine in 1765, industrialization of beer became a reality. Greater attention was also focused on improving brewing methods during the century.

19th Century

The history of beer in the 19th century introduced thermometers and hydrometers led to major improvements in the brewing process. This paved the way for the modern era of brewing - commercial refrigeration, automatic bottling, pasteurization, and railroad distribution.

-Extra: Creation of Oktoberfest in 1810 (world’s largest Volksfest)

20th Century and Beyond

The history of beer in the 20th century saw the laws, technology, scientific advancement, business organization, and many other changes occurred. They influenced brewing techniques, brewery size, drinking tastes, and drinking patterns. Alternate beer such as 0% beer was also introduced.

Social media are a source of exposure to two important factors that offline are associated with alcohol use: - Peer alcohol behaviour - Alcohol advertising

Content posted by adolescenets and young adults likely is seen by peers as well as younger users on social media.

Although restrictions exist to protect young people from exposure to alcohol advertisements on traditional media channels (television programs), adolescenets still have access to alcool advertising in many venue.

Social media present a new venue for alcohol advertisers, particularly because they can target messages and foster connections with consumers. This approach is of particular concern because it can easily reach adlescents and young adults under the legal drinking age.

Economic Impacts

It is found that 1 in every 110 jobs in the world is linked through direct, indirect, or induced impact channels to the beer sector and that its total economic impact amounted to $555 billion of gross value added to the global GDP.

In 2019, the last year before Covid upended the industry, the beer sector supported 23 million jobs from farming to delivery to retail and on-premise sales. The beer industry was severely hit by the pandemic, report suggested that large brewers' revenus declined by around 12%. However, those numbers were improving in 2021.

Besides the industry's support to the global economy, it also helps fund numerous government programs through tax revenues collected. The report estimates that the brewers and their downstream revenue value chain made and supported $262 billion in tax payments. $109 billion was in VAT and excise duties on beer sales.

Technological Impacts

Beer industry is one of te most tech-fueled businesses in the world. Several production-related pieces of tech came to the forefront of the beer landscape.

- Canning: canning beer is a seven-step process that once required significant investment and manpower. A large part of the canning process is dedicated to purging air from the can (and keeping it out). One way is through the use of Nitrogen canning.

- Software becoming a major player in the craft beer segment. A project is called IntelligentX, the service uses AI and machine learning to understand the individual taste of its users.

Sustainability: Eco-friendly production
(1) Brewing Process
(2) Beer Packaging

1) Brewing Process: Sustainability concerns regarding the water use of more than 20:1 litre of brewed beer, energy use and waste generation of 85% of byproducts in the brewing process.

Technological solutions include:
- Water treatment and exploring ways to recycle waste water into beer.
- Repurposing it into food by using wastewater to create single-cell proteins that are suitable for feeding animals like fishes.

2) Beer Packaging: Research on life-cycle and greenhouse gas emissions of beer packaging materials have shown no definite conclusion on whether cans or bottles are environmentally superior. While bottles may be better in ameliorating the heavy impacts of mining bauxite for virgin aluminium to make cans, cans on the other hand, has a higher recycling rate containing approximately 68% recycled content and lower greenhouse gas emissions.
